## Thesis
F&B brands scale when the concept solves a social behavior gap and the economics work long before the brand feels fashionable.

## Summary
Riyaaz Amlani shares how hangout culture became a restaurant business, where Indian cuisine white-space still exists, and what breaks first-time restaurateurs.

## Audience fit
Founders exploring hospitality, restaurants, cafes, or experience-led offline brands.

## Framework
1. Start from the social job to be done and the city's actual behavior, not from menu aesthetics alone.
2. Choose city and micro-location before overbuilding design, because traffic and habit are the first moat.
3. Model the P and L around food cost, rent, overhead, and margin targets before expanding the concept.
4. Scale only after one format shows reliable demand, menu fit, and service consistency.

## High-signal takeaways
- Indian cuisine still has underbuilt chains: There is room to modernize familiar food formats that already have cultural pull and repeat demand.
- Undercapitalization kills more restaurants than lack of taste: Many founders panic because they start too thin and do not have enough runway to iterate the model.
- Menu design is product strategy: What the customer sees and how choices are framed change throughput, economics, and brand memory.

## Market white-space
Modern Indian cuisine chains and hangout-first hospitality formats built for new urban behavior instead of copied Western templates.

## Resource stack
- City demand mapping: Benchmark city behavior, frequency, and spending before placing the first format.
- Menu engineering: Use menu structure to control margin mix, order behavior, and brand recall.
- Food aggregator economics: Treat commissions and digital discoverability as structural inputs, not afterthoughts.
